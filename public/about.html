About

The story behind the practice, and the framework built from it.

Why This Work

Every coaching practice makes an implicit claim: trust me to hold you while you cross. This is why I can.

I became a threshold-holder because I know, from the inside, what the absence of one feels like. For most of my life and career, the belief I ran on was my own. I had managers who didn't see me, environments that asked me to hold space for everyone but myself, and institutions that told me to figure out how to work around it. Nobody held the framework for me, so I learned to build one, hold it myself, and then hold it for others.

I joke that I run my success out of spite. When someone tells me I can't do something, I double down and find a creative way to do it anyway. That joke contains my actual engine: "you can't" converts directly into fuel. It's why my signature move, across every person I've developed, is engineering a path where the org chart says none exists.

And underneath the spite is a quieter philosophy: I was never afraid of my team learning my job. I encouraged it, because I decided early that a strong team was my strength. Where most managers hoard their responsibilities out of fear of being replaced, I hand mine out as a curriculum.

This is what I did for over a decade without a title for it: standing at the threshold with someone, the place between who they've been and who they're becoming, and holding the framework steady while they cross.

The Threshold Framework

The "right person, right seat" lens traces honestly to Jim Collins and Gino Wickman. Their framework is diagnostic: it tells you which quadrant a person occupies. The Threshold Framework is generative: it is the playbook for what to do once you know, built from ten repeatable patterns and eight documented crossings. They tell you to find the right seat; this is how you build one when it doesn't exist.

  • Hire for character, train for skill.
  • Right person, right seat, then build the seat.
  • Delegation is a curriculum.
  • Hold the framework, not the wheel.
  • Coaching should outlast authority.

Those are the principles. How each one plays out in your specific crossing is the work we do together inside an engagement, and it looks different for every person who crosses.

The Parallel Curriculum

This methodology has two laboratories. The first was a decade of leading and developing teams. The second was six years of motherhood, including the hardest kind, through real adversity.

The research done to show up as a good parent (stress response, developmental psychology, emotional coaching) turned out to be leadership research by another name. The same feedback loop that builds a confident kindergartner builds a confident manager. High expectations paired with high support is the quadrant developmental psychology calls authoritative parenting and leadership research calls transformational leadership. Same quadrant, both fields, one sentence:

My expectations are high not because I doubt you, but because I know you can meet them.

Background & Track Record

A Decade of Building Leaders

Over ten years in senior leadership across property management and healthcare operations, including multi-property portfolios and enterprise teams. Six of eight direct reports promoted under her development framework, several into roles that didn't exist until she engineered them.

Eight Documented Case Studies

The framework is written from eight documented crossings, including the honest inverse case where coaching reached its limit. Every pattern in the practice traces to a real person who crossed a real threshold.
